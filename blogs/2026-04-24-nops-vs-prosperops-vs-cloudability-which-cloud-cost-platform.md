The following sections will discuss each category in further detail.  								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-17da60c elementor-widget elementor-widget-dls_table"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<div class="dls-table-wrapper"><div class="dls-table-responsive"><table class="dls-custom-table"><thead><tr><th>Category</th><th>nOps</th><th>ProsperOps</th><th>Apptio Cloudability</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Cost visibility</td><td>Extensive</td><td>Commitment only</td><td>Extensive</td></tr><tr><td>Cost allocation</td><td>Extensive</td><td>Commitment costs only</td><td>Extensive</td></tr><tr><td>Commitment management</td><td>Fully automated</td><td>Fully automated</td><td>Automated</td></tr><tr><td>Automation</td><td>Hourly</td><td>Up to multiple times a day</td><td>Up to multiple times a day</td></tr><tr><td>Multi-cloud</td><td>Yes</td><td>Yes</td><td>Yes</td></tr><tr><td>Kubernetes</td><td>Native visibility &amp; commitments</td><td>No native support</td><td>Visibility + recommendations</td></tr><tr><td>Ease of use</td><td>Lightweight setup</td><td>Straightforward onboarding</td><td>Steeper learning curve</td></tr></tbody></table></div></div>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-6cac2504 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">What These Tools Are Designed For </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-17b96d02 elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Understanding the design philosophy behind each tool explains why they behave so differently in practice.</span></p><p><b>nOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is a full-stack cloud optimization platform built around automation. It covers visibility and commitment management for </span><a href="https://www.nops.io/aws-rate-optimization"><span style="font-weight: 400;">AWS</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, </span><a href="https://www.nops.io/azure-rate-optimization/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Azure</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, and </span><a href="https://www.nops.io/gcp-rate-optimization/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">GCP</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> — including compute and non-compute workloads. Instead of simply identifying waste, nOps is designed to take action continuously: rebalancing commitments hourly to maximize savings and flexibility for teams with fast-changing workloads. </span></p><p><b>ProsperOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> focuses on commitment management. </span><a href="https://www.flexera.com/about-us/press-center/flexera-expands-its-finops-solution-with-agentic-and-ai-enabled-cost-optimization"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Acquired by Flexera in January 2026</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, ProsperOps automates the purchase, exchange, and rebalancing of discount instruments — Reserved Instances (RIs), Savings Plans (SPs), and Committed Use Discounts (CUDs). It has visibility features as well, though these are focused on understanding your commitments (rather than broader visibility, allocation or reporting). </span></p><p><b>Apptio Cloudability</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> (now IBM Cloudability) is primarily a cost visibility and reporting platform. </span><a href="https://newsroom.ibm.com/2023-08-10-IBM-Completes-Acquisition-of-Apptio-Inc"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Acquired by IBM in 2023</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, it provides multi-cloud dashboards, anomaly detection, and cost allocation. Cloudability was named a </span><a href="https://www.apptio.com/resources/analyst-reports/2025-gartner-magic-quadrant-for-cloud-financial-management-tools/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Leader in the 2025 Gartner Magic Quadrant for Cloud Financial Management Tools</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. Its optimization capabilities used to be limited to recommendations, but with its recent acquisition of Cloudwiry, it now has some automated commitment management capabilities. </span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In short: nOps covers both visibility and commitment management, ProsperOps focuses on commitment management only, and Cloudability is strongest in visibility. </span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-1635b855 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Cost Visibility and Reporting: nOps vs ProsperOps vs Cloudability </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-f9577ad elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Cost visibility is where Cloudability has historically been strongest — and where ProsperOps is weakest.</span></p><p><b>Cloudability</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> gives you multi-cloud dashboards, anomaly detection, and customizable reporting across AWS, Azure, and GCP. If your primary need is understanding where money is going, Cloudability&#8217;s reporting engine is mature and well-regarded. The platform supports detailed drill-downs by account, service, region, and tag.</span></p><p><b>nOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> has similar visibility features to Cloudability, but the twist is the AI layer. You get multi-cloud dashboards, anomaly detection, forecasting, and budget management — plus a </span><a href="https://clara.nops.io/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">FinOps AI agent</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. You can use it to interact with your cost data or say commands like “create me a report covering X” and the AI agent will perform the action on your behalf. It also provides visibility into Kubernetes, SaaS tools and AI/GenAI workloads, which neither Cloudability nor ProsperOps covers natively.</span></p><p><b>ProsperOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> offers reporting on commitment performance — Effective Savings Rate (ESR), utilization metrics, and savings attribution through its Intelligent Showback feature. But it lacks broader cost visibility. There are no general-purpose dashboards, no anomaly detection, no budget management, and no forecasting. If you use ProsperOps, you&#8217;ll still need a separate tool for cost visibility. As </span><a href="https://www.reddit.com/r/FinOps/comments/1re603r/who_are_the_real_top_players_in_the_finops_cloud/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">r/FinOps practitioners have noted</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, teams commonly &#8220;combine a commitments layer (ProsperOps or similar) with a cost attribution layer&#8221; — which means two vendors, two contracts, and two interfaces.</span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-3544a7c elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Cost Allocation and Unit Economics: nOps vs ProsperOps vs Cloudability </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-1363c8ff elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Cost allocation — figuring out which team, project, or product is responsible for which portion of the bill — is traditionally one of the most painful challenges in cloud financial management. It gets especially complicated when commitments are purchased centrally but benefits are shared across accounts.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">One Director of Engineering we spoke with described the confusion: they could see that a project saved about $28K in a given month, but couldn&#8217;t untangle how centrally-purchased CUDs were being applied across projects in the same billing account. This is a universal pain point in multi-account environments.</span></p><p><b>Cloudability</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> handles cost allocation well. Its showback and chargeback capabilities let you attribute costs across business units, and its multi-cloud support means you can consolidate allocation data from AWS, Azure, and GCP in one place. It&#8217;s one of the platform&#8217;s core strengths.</span></p><p><b>nOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> also has automated tagging, showback/chargeback, and unit economics down to the container and service level. If you&#8217;re running Kubernetes workloads, nOps can allocate costs by namespace, deployment, or service — not just by AWS account or tag. It also tracks COGS (cost of goods sold) for true unit economics, which is increasingly important for SaaS companies reporting gross margins to boards and investors.</span></p><p><b>ProsperOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> provides Intelligent Showback for commitment-specific costs and savings, allocating them back to accounts or teams. It&#8217;s useful for understanding who benefits from centrally-managed commitments, but it doesn&#8217;t cover broader cost allocation. You won&#8217;t get showback on compute, storage, or container costs through ProsperOps alone.</span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-182f7027 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Commitment Management Capabilities: nOps vs ProsperOps vs Cloudability </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-1263a823 elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This is the category where nOps and ProsperOps compete head-to-head.</span></p><p><b>Cloudability</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> Cloudability offers commitment recommendations and, through Cloudability Savings Automation, can automate parts of the RI and Savings Plan lifecycle. That makes it stronger than a pure visibility tool: it can help identify opportunities, optimize coverage, and reduce the manual work of managing commitments. But these capabilities, based on the Cloudwiry acquisition, are less mature than nOps or ProsperOps. </span></p><p><b>ProsperOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> automates commitment management through its Autonomous Discount Management (ADM) engine. It continuously adjusts your portfolio of RIs, Savings Plans, and CUDs based on actual usage patterns. ProsperOps supports AWS, Azure, and GCP (with multi-cloud support expanded after </span><a href="https://www.flexera.com/more/ProsperOps-Chaos-Genius"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Flexera&#8217;s acquisition</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">). The platform handles purchasing, exchanging, and rebalancing without manual intervention. </span></p><p><b>nOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> also fully automates multicloud commitment management, but with a few key differences. First, nOps rebalances commitments hourly rather than at a less frequent cadence, which captures more incremental savings as workloads shift throughout the day. Competitors typically find ~20% additional savings when switching from competitors. It also focuses on maximizing flexibility in addition to savings. Instead of making large one-time commitment bets, it breaks decisions into small, incremental purchases to shrink risk and commitment periods — particularly valuable as AI accelerates the pace of infrastructure change. </span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-df0b180 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Multi-Cloud, Kubernetes, and AI Support: nOps vs ProsperOps vs Cloudability </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-471ad6d elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Multi-cloud support, Kubernetes cost management, and AI workload visibility are increasingly non-negotiable for growing engineering organizations. nOps has the broadest coverage here.</span></p><p><b>Multi-cloud</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: All three platforms support AWS, Azure, and GCP to some degree. Cloudability has supported multi-cloud visibility since its early days — it&#8217;s a core strength. ProsperOps now covers multi-cloud commitment automation following the Flexera acquisition (historically it was AWS-focused). nOps includes both visibility and cost optimization for AWS, Azure, GCP, Kubernetes and AI. And it covers both compute and non-compute, including database, security, etc.</span></p><p><b>Kubernetes</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: This is where the platforms diverge sharply. Cloudability provides Kubernetes cost visibility through IBM-acquired </span><a href="https://www.kubecost.com/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Kubecost</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, offering allocation and recommendations — but no automated execution. ProsperOps has no native Kubernetes support at all. nOps provides native EKS visibility down to the node, container, and service level.</span></p><p><b>AI workloads</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: AI spend is rapidly becoming a board-level concern. We read an article yesterday about how </span><a href="https://aimagazine.com/news/why-uber-has-already-burned-through-its-ai-budget"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Uber has already burned their entire AI budget for the year</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">. It’s a wake-up call for finance teams. nOps provides visibility and commitments for AI and </span><a href="https://www.nops.io/blog/genai-cost-optimization-the-essential-guide/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">GenAI</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> workloads, including GPU cost tracking. </span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-3bda6d50 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Ease of Implementation and Time to Value: nOps vs ProsperOps vs Cloudability </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-2d187373 elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">How quickly you get value from a platform matters — especially when the CFO is asking why the cloud bill hasn&#8217;t improved yet.</span></p><p><b>nOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> is designed for fast deployment. You can connect your AWS account and start seeing data within minutes. The automation-first approach means the platform starts delivering savings quickly without requiring a dedicated FinOps team to implement recommendations. nOps&#8217;s setup process involves granting IAM permissions, and the platform handles the rest. </span></p><p><b>ProsperOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> also onboards quickly for commitment management. You connect billing data, define preferences, and ADM starts optimizing. The lightweight footprint and narrow focus mean there&#8217;s less to configure. But because ProsperOps only covers commitments, teams typically need to onboard a second (or third) tool for visibility and broader optimization — which adds complexity back.</span></p><p><b>Cloudability</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> has a steeper implementation curve. </span><a href="https://www.g2.com/products/ibm-cloudability/reviews"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Users on G2</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> report navigation challenges and a learning curve. The platform is enterprise-grade, which means more configuration, more roles to define, and more dashboards to build before you&#8217;re getting actionable insights. Now that Cloudability is part of IBM, onboarding may involve enterprise sales cycles and contract negotiations that don&#8217;t suit smaller or mid-market teams.</span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-bc1e884 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 					<h2 class="elementor-heading-title elementor-size-default">Pricing and ROI: nOps vs ProsperOps vs Cloudability </h2>				</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-92966a7 elementor-widget elementor-widget-text-editor"> 				<div class="elementor-widget-container"> 									<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Pricing models differ significantly across these three platforms, and the model you choose affects your ROI calculation.</span></p><p><b>Cloudability</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> uses tiered annual contracts based on managed cloud spend. According to </span><a href="https://holori.com/apptio-cloudability-pricing-in-2025-and-alternatives/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">AWS Marketplace listings</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, pricing starts at approximately $30,000 per year for up to $1M in managed cloud spend, scaling to $76,680 for $3M and $132,480 for $6M. Overage fees apply if you exceed contracted limits. This is a fixed enterprise-level cost regardless of whether you act on the platform&#8217;s recommendations.</span></p><p><b>ProsperOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> charges a </span><a href="https://www.prosperops.com/pricing/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">savings-share fee</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> — a percentage of the savings it generates through commitment optimization. The exact percentage is contract-specific and tiered. This aligns incentives: ProsperOps only earns when you save. For workload-level optimization (Scheduler), ProsperOps charges a flat fee per managed resource per month.</span></p><p><b>nOps</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> offers both a savings-share model and a flat-fee option. For commitment management, nOps takes a portion of realized savings — similar to ProsperOps but applied across a broader optimization surface (commitments + compute + containers + storage). There’s no upfront cost or long-term commitment required. The key differentiator: nOps only charges for additional results it delivers. If you have pre-existing commitments or baseline savings, nOps won’t charge for any of that. In contrast, </span><a href="https://www.nops.io/blog/prosperops-pricing/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">ProsperOps pricing</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> can include categories like inherited, base, and smart savings, and contract terms can define which categories are billable. According to their </span><a href="https://www.prosperops.com/pricing/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">docs</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, if ProsperOps-managed discount instruments are terminated before their term expires, unrealized Savings Share charges may be due for the remaining term.</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Here&#8217;s what this means in practice. With Cloudability, you&#8217;re paying $30K-$130K+ annually for dashboards and recommendations. Your actual savings depend on whether your team has the bandwidth and expertise to execute. With ProsperOps, you pay a share of commitment savings — but you&#8217;ll still need a separate tool (and budget) for everything else. With nOps, you get both in a single platform. </span></p>								</div> 				</div> 				<div class="elementor-element elementor-element-3a7feb9 elementor-widget elementor-widget-heading"> 				<div
